Connecting the maths to the money

A MAPE of 4.8% means that on a typical €2,400 monthly spend our model's average error is approximately €115. That is precise enough to be genuinely actionable. The March 2025 high-risk flag is not a guess — it is the model's AR component detecting momentum from a high-spend February, compounded by the seasonal pattern learned from March 2022 and 2023 in the training data. Cutting €300 from discretionary categories that month brings forecasted spend back below income and eliminates the deficit risk.

Regression findings

The OLS model finds that number of transactions is the strongest predictor of monthly spending (p < 0.001) — each additional transaction adds approximately €8 to the monthly total. The December dummy is highly significant (p = 0.003), confirming a seasonal Christmas spike of approximately €340 above baseline. Average transaction size is not statistically significant at the 5% level, suggesting that spending frequency — not individual transaction size — is the primary driver of monthly overspend. The practical implication: alert users when transaction count is trending high mid-month.

Machine learning findings

Three classifiers were trained and compared using 5-fold cross-validation: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, and Random Forest. Random Forest achieved the highest accuracy of 87.5% and an AUC of 0.91, well above the Klarna credit risk benchmark of 0.85. The model was trained on a binary target — months where expenses exceeded income were labelled 1 (at risk). The most important predictive feature is number of transactions, consistent with the regression finding. This classifier is what powers the overdraft risk alerts on the Forecast page.

Volatility findings

The ARCH-LM test confirms that ARCH effects are present in monthly spending residuals (p < 0.05), meaning spending volatility is not constant — it clusters. The GARCH(1,1) model estimates alpha + beta = 0.94, indicating highly persistent shocks. In practical terms: if December was unusually volatile, January will likely be too. AFOS uses the GARCH-forecasted conditional variance to dynamically widen confidence intervals in uncertain months — giving users a more honest picture of forecast uncertainty.
